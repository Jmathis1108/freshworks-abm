The Challenge

When every ticket is a different person,
product, country, and urgency level

Uber is not one product. It is rides, deliveries, freight, and business travel, in 72 countries, with two customer types per trip and a safety obligation that demands instant escalation every time.

ğŸ”„

Drivers and riders have opposite versions of the same incident

A fare dispute looks completely different from the driver's side than the rider's. A payment complaint might be a rider unhappy with the charge or a driver who has not been paid. Without unified context, agents are resolving half a story and making decisions on incomplete information.

ğŸš¨

Safety reports cannot wait in a general queue

A rider reporting a safety concern mid-trip is a different category of urgency than a billing question. If it lands in the same inbox with no priority routing, the consequences are severe. Safety escalation has to be automatic, immediate, and tracked to resolution without exception.

ğŸŒ

72 countries means 72 different regulatory and cultural contexts

What Uber can offer a rider in the US differs from what it can offer in Brazil, India, or Germany. Dispute resolution processes, refund policies, and data handling requirements vary by market. Agents need city-level context on every ticket, not a generic global playbook.

ğŸ”

Rides, Eats, and Business are three different products in one queue

An Uber Eats complaint about a missing item, a ride dispute about a cancelled trip, and an Uber for Business expense query are completely different workflows. Siloed queues mean duplicated infrastructure. A shared queue with no routing intelligence means every agent handles everything badly.

Meet Freddy AI

AI that knows whether it is
a driver, a rider, or a safety report

Most AI support tools route tickets based on keywords. Freddy AI routes based on context. When a ticket comes in, Freddy reads the account type, the product line, the market, the trip history, and the issue nature before a single agent sees it.

A driver pay dispute goes to the earnings resolution team with the full trip log already loaded. A rider safety report bypasses the queue entirely and escalates to the safety team in seconds. An Uber Eats missing item complaint goes to the restaurant resolution flow with the order confirmed. Right ticket, right person, right context, every time.

People-first AI means drivers feel heard about their earnings, riders feel safe reporting incidents, and the agent has everything they need to resolve it fast. That is what trust in a two-sided marketplace looks like.
